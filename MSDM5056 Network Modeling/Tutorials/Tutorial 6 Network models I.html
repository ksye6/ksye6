We are often interested in three properties of a network:

  1. its degree distribution $P_k$ (and its moments $\langle{k}\rangle$ and $\langle{k^2}\rangle$),
  2. its average clustering coefficient $C$, and
  3. its average shortest path length $L$.

A real network tends to have a scale-free $P_k$, a high $C$, and a short $L$ (i.e. the small-world property). In the coming three tutorials, we will learn various network models that try to capture these properties.

Let $N$ be the size of the following networks.

In [2]:
def kCL(G):
    k = np.mean([k for n, k in G.degree])
    C = nx.average_clustering(G)
    L = nx.average_shortest_path_length(G)
    return '<k> = %.3g, C = %.3g, and L = %.3g.' % (k, C, L)

1. Regular networks¶

A $K$-regular network has the same degree $K$ for all nodes, so $P_k=\delta{\left(k-K\right)}$. In addition to $K$, its properties also depend on its dimension.

1.1 Rings¶

A ring is $2$-regular and $1$-dimensional. (It is just a line with the two ends conencted.) You can construct it with cycle_graph.

If we additionally connect each node to its $\left(K2\right)$ nearest neighbours, the resultant network is $K$-regular. While it does not have a proper name, we may call it a $K$-regular ring.

In [3]:
def K_ring(N, K):
    # assuming K is even for simplicity
    if K<=2: return nx.cycle_graph(N)
    ring = nx.empty_graph(N)
    for n in range(N):
        ring.add_edges_from([(n, (n+i+1)%N) for i in range(K//2)])
        ring.add_edges_from([(n, (n-i-1)%N) for i in range(K//2)])
    return ring

fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(7, 2))
fig.suptitle('$K$-regular rings', y=1.15)
for K, col in zip([2, 4, 6], ax):

    col.set_title('$K=%d$' % K)
    nx.draw_circular(ring, ax=col, node_size=75, node_color='b',
                     width=.5, linewidths=.5, edgecolors='k')

We can prove that $ C= \dfrac{3}{4} \dfrac{K-2}{K-1}$ if $K$ remains less than $\dfrac{2}{3}N$. In contrast, $L\approx \dfrac{N}{2K} \Rightarrow L \sim N$ for all $K$. Hence, the nodes on a general ring are highly clustered and close to each other.

Complete network.¶

A complete network is fully connected, so it is $\left(N-1\right)$-regular. In some sense, we may regard it as an $\left(N-1\right)$-regular ring. It is the densest among all networks of the same size. It has $C=1$ and $L=1$ because all nodes are each other's neighbours.

You can create it with complete_graph.

In [5]:
complete = nx.complete_graph(10)
print('A %d-node complete graph has ' % len(complete) + kCL(complete))

plt.figure(figsize=(2, 2))
nx.draw_circular(complete, node_size=100, node_color='b',
                 width=.5, linewidths=.5, edgecolors='k')

A 10-node complete graph has <k> = 9, C = 1, and L = 1.

A complete network is $\left(N-1\right)$-dimensional according to mathematicians' arguments. Since a ring resembles a complete network once $K$ approaches $N$, we can deduce that a ring's dimension grows from $1$ to $\left(N-1\right)$ as $K$ increases. We may also define $K=\dfrac{2}{3}N$ as the critical point between a ring and a complete network because of the change in $C$.

1.2 Regular lattices¶

Without delving into its confusing and abstract definition in mathematics, a lattice is basically just a grid. A regular lattice is a grid on which all nodes have the same degree. (Here are examples of *irregular* lattices.)

Two-dimensional lattices.¶

The most common form of a $2$-dimensional regular lattice is a square lattice. You can create it with grid_2d_graph. It has $C=0$ (obviously) and $L\sim N^{1/2}$.

Despite the same $K=4$, a square lattice and a $4$-regular ring possess quite different $C$ and $L$ because of the difference in their dimensions: the former is strictly $2$-dimensional, while the latter's dimension is somewhat larger than $1$ (and arguably almost $1$ for a large $N$). From this simple comparison you can see the significance of a network's dimension.

Hypercubic lattices.¶

If you duplicate a $2$-node line and connect each node to its counterpart, you get a $4$-node square. If you duplicate the square and connect each node to its counterpart, you get a $8$-node cube. A hypercube generalizes this idea: you get a $\left(d+1\right)$-dimensional hypercube by joining two $d$-dimensional hypercubes, which have $2d$ vertices each. A line, a square, and a cube are respectively $1$-, $2$-, and $3$-dimensional hypercubes. You can construct a hypercube with hypercube_graph.

A hypercubic lattice is a lattice whose unit cell is a hypercube, so a ring, a square lattice, and a cubic lattice are respectively $1$-, $2$-, and $3$-dimensional hypercubic lattices. More generally, the unit cell of a hypercubic lattice is allowed to be a hyperrectangle, which has different lengths on each side. You can create such a hyperrectangular lattice with grid_graph.

A $d$-dimensional periodic hypercubic lattice possesses $L\sim N^{1/d}$. In fact, a high-dimensional network usually yields a short $L$.

The meaning of "lattice".¶

So what is a "lattice" actually? In case you wonder, mathematicians define it in an abstract way that laymen can hardly understand. (Reading Wikipedia may not be so helpful this time.)

Informally speaking, the nodes on a lattice are so regularly arranged that the lattice looks self-repeating, and the edges on it only conenct nodes that are "close" enough with respect to some kind of "distance". (A ring is therefore sometimes referred to as a "ring lattice".) Here I am illustrating the second idea with the Euclidean distance between two nodes, but the resultant networks are not lattices because of the irregular arrangement of their nodes.

2. Random networks¶

A random network may be defined in terms of its number of edges or connection probability, which Erds and Rnyi (1958) and Gilbert (1959) proposed respectively.

  • In the Erds-Rnyi (ER) model, a random network contains $N$ nodes, and $E$ out of the $E_0=\left(\matrix{N\\2}\right)$ possible edges are drawn to be realized.
  • In the Gilbert model, a random network contains $N$ nodes, and each possible edge is realized with probability $p$. This is also called the binomial model.

The two models are statistically similar but distinct. While an ER network definitely has $E$ edges, the number of edges on a Gilbert network fluctuates around $\langle{E}\rangle=pE_0$. The probability that a Gilbert network has exactly $E$ edges reads

\begin{align} P_E = \left(\matrix{E_0\\E}\right) p^E \left(1-p\right)^{E_0-E}\,. \end{align}

2.1 The Erdős-Rényi model¶

Nowadays, we somehow only pay tribute to Erds and Rnyi while sticking to Gilbert's formulation. In NetworkX, erdos_renyi_graph refers to the Gilbert model, so does gnp_random_graph. You can call the original ER model with gnm_random_graph.

In addition, the time complexity of another function fast_gnp_random_graph is $O{\left(N+E\right)}$, whereas erdos_renyi_graph costs $O{\left(N^2\right)}$. If the desired network is sparse, i.e. $E\ll E_0$, the former function is much faster than the latter.

In [11]:
er_func = nx.erdos_renyi_graph     # O(N^2)
er_fast = nx.fast_gnp_random_graph # O(N+E)

Degree distribution.¶

The ER network's degree distribution follows a binomial distribution

\begin{align} P_k = \left(\matrix{N-1\\k}\right) p^k \left(1-p\right)^{N-1-k}\,. \end{align}

If $k \ll N$, the first term approaches $\dfrac{\left(N-1\right)^k}{k!}$ and the third term reduces to $e^{-\langle{k}\rangle}$ because

\begin{align} \ln{\big[\left(1-p\right)^{N-1-k}\big]} &= \left(N-1-k\right)\ln{\left(1-\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle}{N-1}\right)} \\ &\approx -\left(N-1-k\right)\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle}{N-1} & \left(N\gg\langle{k}\rangle\right) \\ &= -\langle{k}\rangle\left(1-\dfrac{k}{N-1}\right) \\ &\approx -\langle{k}\rangle\,. \end{align}

Hence, if $N\gg\langle{k}\rangle$, the binomial distribution becomes a Poisson distribution

\begin{align} P_k \stackrel{N\gg\langle{k}\rangle}{=} \dfrac{\left(N-1\right)^k}{k!} p^k e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} = \dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle^k}{k!} e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} \,, \end{align}

where $\langle{k}\rangle=p\left(N-1\right)\approx pN$.

Consider the binomial form of $P_k$. Its second moment reads

\begin{align} \langle{k^2}\rangle &= p\left(1-p\right)\left(N-1\right) + p^2\left(N-1\right)^2 \\ &\approx p\left(1-p\right) N + \left(pN\right)^2\,, \end{align}

so the variance in degree is

\begin{align} \sigma_k^2 &= \langle{k^2}\rangle-\langle{k}\rangle^2 \\ &= p\left(1-p\right)\left(N-1\right) \\ &\approx p\left(1-p\right)N\,. \end{align}

As $N$ grows, $P_k$ approaches a Poisson distribution, which turns out to have $\langle{k^2}\rangle = \langle{k}\rangle^2+\langle{k}\rangle$.

\begin{align} \langle{k^2}\rangle &= \sum_{k\geq0} {k^2 \dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle^k}{k!} e^{-\langle{k}\rangle}} \\ &= \langle{k}\rangle e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} \sum_{k\geq1} {k \dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle^{k-1}}{\left(k-1\right)!}} \\ &= \langle{k}\rangle e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} \Bigg[\sum_{k\geq1} {\left(k-1\right) \dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle^{k-1}}{\left(k-1\right)!}} +\sum_{k\geq1} {\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle^{k-1}}{\left(k-1\right)!}}\Bigg] \\ &= \langle{k}\rangle e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} \Bigg[\langle{k}\rangle \sum_{k\geq2} {\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle^{k-2}}{\left(k-2\right)!}} +\sum_{k\geq1} {\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle^{k-1}}{\left(k-1\right)!}}\Bigg] \\ &= \langle{k}\rangle e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} \left(\langle{k}\rangle e^{\langle{k}\rangle}+e^{\langle{k}\rangle}\right) & \left(e^x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{x^n}{n!}}\right)\\ &= \langle{k}\rangle^2+\langle{k}\rangle \end{align}

Hence the variance in degree simply reduces to $\sigma_k^2 = \langle{k}\rangle$.

Clustering coefficient.¶

A node's clustering coefficient is the probability of its neighbours' connection. On an ER network, the probability is homogeneously $p$ because all edges are random, so all nodes are expected to have a clustering coeffcient $c=p$. This means that $C\equiv\langle{c}\rangle=p=\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle}{N-1}$.

If $\langle{k}\rangle$ is fixed, $C$ drops with the network's size as $C\sim N^{-1}$. This prediction does not match empirical observations, though.

Shortest path length.¶

An ER network has an incredibly short average shortest path length $L\approx\dfrac{\ln{N}}{\ln{\langle{k}\rangle}}$. In contrast to hypercubic lattices, $L$ increases logarithmically with $N$ instead of exponentially, so it remains short even if $N$ is enormous.

Suppose that our world is a random network with $7\times10^9$ nodes. If we all have $50$ friends, then the average interpersonal distance is around $6$ despite our world's enormous size. This is the origin of "six degrees of separation", a famous term in popular science.

In general, a network is said to possess the "small-world property" if it shows $L\sim{\ln{N}}$.

2.2 Percolation¶

When we are given a configuration $\left(N,p\right)$, we naturally ask how connected the resultant ER network is. This question turns out to be closely related to percolation theory, which studies the spread of a substance through a medium.

For example, a sponge with $N$ holes may be modelled as a network. If a hole absorbs water and passes its to an adjacent hole, thus soaking the substance in between, with probability $p$, how wet do we expect the sponge to become eventually?

Giant component.¶

A giant component $g$ is a component whose size is comparable to the entire network. Practically, we may regard a network's uniquely largest component as the giant component if its size breaks above a certain threshold.

If a giant component exists, a node either belongs to or does not belong to the component. Let $N_g$ be the number of nodes inside the giant component, then its fractional size is $S\equiv\dfrac{N_g}{N}$, whereas the fraction of nodes outside it is $u\equiv1-S$. Given that node $i$ is outside the giant component, either statement below is true for it and another node $j$.

  1. $i$ is not connected with $j$. This happens with probability $1-p$.
  2. $i$ is connected with $j$, but $j$ is also outside the component. This happens with probability $pu$.

Because there are $N-1$ choices for $j$, the probability that $i$ is outside the giant component reads $\left(1-p+pu\right)^{N-1}$. Meanwhile, the fraction of nodes outside the component is just the probability for a node to be outside it, so $u=\left(1-p+pu\right)^{N-1}$.

\begin{align} \therefore \ln{u} &= \left(N-1\right) \ln{\big[1-p\left(1-u\right)\big]} \\ &= \left(N-1\right) \ln{\bigg[1-\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle}{N-1}\left(1-u\right)\bigg]} \\ &\approx -\left(N-1\right) \dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle}{N-1}\left(1-u\right) & \left(N\gg\langle{k}\rangle\right) \\ &= -\langle{k}\rangle\left(1-u\right) \\ \end{align}

This implies $u=e^{-\langle{k}\rangle\left(1-u\right)}$ and thus

\begin{align} S= 1-e^{-\langle{k}\rangle S}\,. \end{align}

The giant component's size depends on $\langle{k}\rangle$. The equation only has a trivial solution $S=0$ for $\langle{k}\rangle\in\left[0, 1\right)$, but a non-trivial solution emerges once $\langle{k}\rangle>1$. Therefore,

\begin{align} \langle{k}\rangle=1 \end{align}

marks the phase transition from the absence to the presence of a giant component.

In terms of sponge and water, we expect to see a mostly wet sponge once $p$ exceeds $N^{-1}$.

    Ss = np.linspace(Ss[argmin-1], upper, 100)
    return Ss[np.argmin(np.abs(1-np.exp(-k*Ss)-Ss))]
Ss_theory = np.array([solve_S_with(k) for k in ks])

Connectedness.¶

We know that $\langle{k}\rangle>1$ implies the existence of a giant component. A follow-up question asks how large $\langle{k}\rangle$ should at least be so that the ER network is connected, i.e. its giant component is the only component and thus the entire network.

If there are $N_g$ nodes in the giant component, the probability that a node falls outside it is $\left(1-p\right)^{N_g}$, which becomes $\left(1-p\right)^N$ as we focus on the transition to the connected regime $N_g\to N$. Since $\lim\limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} {\left(1+\dfrac{x}{n}\right)^n} = e^x$, the probability can be simplified to

\begin{align} \left(1-p\right)^N = \left(1-\dfrac{\langle{k}\rangle}{N}\right)^N \stackrel{N\rightarrow\infty}{=} e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} \end{align}

when $N$ is large. Therefore, the probability that an ER network is connected, i.e. all nodes are inside the giant component, reads $\left( 1-e^{-\langle{k}\rangle} \right)^N$. It is the expected frequency to observe a connected ER network.

Meanwhile, $Ne^{-\langle{k}\rangle}$ nodes are expected to fall outside the giant component. By requiring less than one node falls outside it, we get

\begin{align} Ne^{-\langle{k}\rangle} < 1\quad \Rightarrow\quad\langle{k}\rangle > \ln{N} \quad \Rightarrow\quad p > \dfrac{\ln{N}}{N} \,. \end{align}

In terms of sponge and water, we expect to see a completely wet sponge if $p > \dfrac{\ln{N}}{N}$.

2.3 The configuration model¶

Instead of specifying the number of edges or connection probability, we may choose to specify a random network's degree distribution or degree sequence. The two approaches yield the generalized random model and the configuration model.

  • The generalized random model controls a network's degree distribution $P_k$ by defining a generating function $g$. This is an abstract formulation, but it provides us some novel perspectives to analyse networks. Read Newman's Chapter 13 and Albert and Barabsi's Section V (2002) if you feel interested.
\begin{align} P_k \equiv \dfrac{1}{k!} g^{\left(k\right)}{\left(0\right)}\, \end{align}
  • The configuration model specifies a network's degree sequence $\left\{k\right\}$ instead. It is computationally simpler and more practical. It resembles Erds and Rnyi's original $G{\left(N, E\right)}$ model because they both fix the the number of edges on a network.

Now I will focus on the configuration model.

Algorithm.¶

Given a degree sequence $\left\{k\right\}$, the configuration model creates a network in the following steps.

  1. Place $k_i$ stubs (i.e. "half-edges") at node $i$.
  2. Randomly pick two stubs and join them as an edge.
  3. Repeat step 2 until all stubs are paired up.

$\left\{k\right\}$ is graphical if it can create a simple graph (i.e. a graph without multi-edges or self-loops). Obviously, $\sum_i{k_i}$ must be even for a graphical $\left\{k\right\}$, otherwise one stub will be left unpaired. A graphical $\left\{k\right\}$ also satisfies the Erds-Gallai theorem.

You can use is_graphical to check whether $\left\{k\right\}$ is graphical, then you can turn it into a graph with configuration_model, which returns MultiGraph by default.

Edge probability.¶

Consider a simple random network first. How likely are nodes $i$ and $j$ connected if they have degrees $k_i$ and $k_j$? We have solved this problem when we discussed modularity, and the answer is $p_{ij}=\dfrac{k_ik_j}{2E}$.

\begin{align} p_{ij} &= P\left(i\text{ joins }j\right) \\ &= k_i \times P\left(\text{a specific stub of }i\text{ joins }j\right) \\\ &= k_i \times k_j \times P\left(\text{a specific stub of }i\text{ joins a specific stub of }j\right) \\ &= k_i \times k_j \times \dfrac{1}{2E-1} \approx \dfrac{k_ik_j}{2E} \end{align}

Probability of multi-edges.¶

We can similarly calculate the probability $p^\left(2\right)_{ij}$ that $i$ and $j$ are connected with two edges.

\begin{align} p^\left(2\right) _ {ij} &= P\left(\text{two stubs of }i\text{ joins two stubs of }j\right) \\ &= \dfrac{k_ik_j}{2E} \times P\left[\text{one of }i\text{'s ($k_i-1$) free stubs joins one of }j\text{'s ($k_j-1$) free stubs}\right] \\ &= \dfrac{k_ik_j}{2E} \times \dfrac{\left(k_i-1\right)\left(k_j-1\right)}{2E} = \dfrac{k_ik_j\left(k_i-1\right)\left(k_j-1\right)}{\left(2E\right)^2} \end{align}

In general, the probability of having $n$ multi-edges between two nodes is proportional to $E^{-n}$, so it vanishes exponentially. We can therefore ignore cases in which two nodes are connected with more than two edges, meaning that we only expect there are $m=\dfrac{1}{2}\sum_i{\sum_j{p^\left(2\right)_{ij}}}$ multi-edges in total.

\begin{align} m &= \dfrac{1}{2}\sum_i\sum_j{\dfrac{k_ik_j\left(k_i-1\right)\left(k_j-1\right)}{\left(2E\right)^2}} \\ &= \dfrac{1}{2\langle{k}\rangle^2} \sum_i{\dfrac{k_i\left(k_i-1\right)}{N}} \sum_j{\dfrac{k_j\left(k_j-1\right)}{N}} & \left(2E=N\langle{k\rangle}\right) \\ &= \dfrac{1}{2} \left(\dfrac{\langle{k^2}\rangle-\langle{k}\rangle}{\langle{k}\rangle}\right)^2 \end{align}

Note that $m$ does not depend on $N$. When $N$ grows at fixed $\langle{k}\rangle$ and $\langle{k^2}\rangle$, the finite number of multi-edges will be shared by more nodes, so the probability for a node to have multi-edges is extremely small. In other words, when $N$ is large, the configuration model probably creates a simple network.

(By the way, the ratio $\dfrac{\langle{k^2}\rangle-\langle{k}\rangle}{\langle{k}\rangle}$ will appear again in the following derivations.)

Probability of self-loops.¶

The $k_i$ stubs of node $i$ can form $\left(\matrix{k_i \\ 2}\right)$ possible pairs among themselves. Since each specific pair of stubs joins with a probability $\dfrac{1}{2E-1}$, the probability for $i$ to have self-loops is

\begin{align} p_{ii} = \left(\matrix{k_i \\ 2}\right) \times \dfrac{1}{2E-1} \approx \dfrac{k_i\left(k_i-1\right)}{4E}\,. \end{align}

Again, the probability for a node to have $n$ self-loops scales with $E^{-n}$ and gets insignificant quickly, so the expected number of self-loops summing $p_{ii}$ is

\begin{align} s = \sum_i {p_{ii}} = \sum_i {\dfrac{k_i\left(k_i-1\right)}{4E}} = \dfrac{1}{2} \dfrac{\langle{k^2}\rangle-{\langle{k}\rangle}}{\langle{k}\rangle}\,. \end{align}

Because it is independent of $N$, the effect of self-loops on the configuration model also vanishes as $N$ grows.

Clustering coefficient.¶

Since a node's edge connects itself to a specific $k$-degree node with probability $\dfrac{k}{2E-1}\approx\dfrac{k}{2E}$, the probability for the node to connect with a $k$-degree node is $\dfrac{k}{2E}\times{NP_k} = \dfrac{kP_k}{\langle{k}\rangle}\equiv p_k$.

Now consider three nodes $h$, $i$, and $j$ that are trying to form a triplet. First, $h$ links to both $i$ and $j$ with probability $p_{k_i}p_{k_j}$. Then, both $i$ and $j$ have already spent one stub for $h$, so the probability for them to connect reduces to $\dfrac{\left(k_i-1\right)\left(k_j-1\right)}{2E}$. Combining these statements, the configuration model's clustering coefficient reads

\begin{align} C &= \sum_{k_i=1}^\infty \sum_{k_j=1}^\infty {p_{k_i}p_{k_j}\dfrac{\left(k_i-1\right)\left(k_j-1\right)}{2E}} \\ &= \dfrac{1}{2E} \sum_{k_i=1}^\infty \sum_{k_j=1}^\infty {p_{k_i}p_{k_j}\left(k_i-1\right)\left(k_j-1\right)} \\ &= \dfrac{1}{2E} \left[ \sum_{k=1}^\infty {\left(k-1\right)p_k}\right ]^2 = \dfrac{1}{2E\langle{k}\rangle^2} \left[\sum_{k=1}^\infty {\left(k-1\right)kP_k}\right]^2 \\ &= \dfrac{1}{N\langle{k}\rangle^3} \Big(\langle{k^2}\rangle-\langle{k}\rangle\Big)^2 = \dfrac{1}{N\langle{k}\rangle} \left(\dfrac{\langle{k^2}\rangle-{\langle{k}\rangle}}{\langle{k}\rangle}\right)^2\,. \end{align}

Despite a different formulation from the ER network, the configuration model also yields $C\sim N^{-1}$ and violates empirical observations. This tells us again that real networks are not formed at random.

Degree-preserving randomization.¶

If you desire to prevent $\left\{k\right\}$ from being distorted by any multi-edges or self-loops, degree-preserving randomization is an alternative way to realize the configuration model.

Given a network that follows $\left\{k\right\}$, the process iteratively chooses two arbitrary edges $\left(u,v\right)$ and $\left(x,y\right)$ and swaps their ends, resulting in $\left(u,x\right)$ and $\left(v,y\right)$. Obviously, a swap preserves the nodes' degrees. If the swap results in multi-edges or self-loops, the swap is discarded.

You may carry out the step with double_edge_swap or connected_double_edge_swap, which ensures the connectedness of the randomized network. Beware that the functions modify a network in-place, so save the original network if you need to reuse it.

More importantly, degree-preserving randomization nullifies all the equations derived above, but the deviation diminishes as $N$ grows.
